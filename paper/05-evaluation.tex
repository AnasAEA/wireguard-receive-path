\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup and Methodology}
\label{sec:eval-method}

\paragraph{Testbed.}
The experiments use two bare-metal CloudLab c220g2 nodes: one load generator and
one instrumented receiver.  Each node has two Intel Xeon E5-2660 v3 sockets
(20 physical cores and 40 hardware threads in total) and an Intel X520 10-GbE
interface.  The receiver exposes 40 RX queues through the \texttt{ixgbe}
driver.  It runs Ubuntu 22.04 with Linux 5.15.0-177 and an out-of-tree
WireGuard module rebuilt from pristine Ubuntu 5.15 source plus the five-file
implementation overlay described in Section~\ref{sec:design}.  The final
work-stealing campaigns use module source version
\texttt{40814CD3CA80D0908BF87D3}.

The generator creates network namespaces and WireGuard peers, sends through the
physical link, and drives receiver-side \texttt{iperf3} servers.  Multi-tunnel
experiments use distinct UDP ports; the confirmation experiments use four
parallel TCP streams inside one WireGuard tunnel.  Thus the latter remains one
outer five-tuple even though its inner workload contains several streams.

\paragraph{Treatments.}
We use a $2\times2$ treatment structure in the final campaigns.  \emph{Off}
disables the wake changes and stealing.  \emph{Both} enables
\texttt{wg\_supp} and \texttt{wg\_headwake}.  \emph{Steal4} enables only
\texttt{wg\_steal=4}; \emph{bsteal4} combines both wake changes with steal4.
Injected decrypt delay and the earlier timer-based trigger are disabled.
Gate A disables diagnostic counters; Gate B enables them for descriptive
mechanism accounting.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Throughput is received \texttt{iperf3} goodput in the uncapped experiment.
The matched-load experiment instead computes delivered rate from the change in
\texttt{wg0} receive bytes over exactly the CPU measurement interval.  CPU
snapshots come from \texttt{/proc/stat} for all 40 logical CPUs.  We report
\emph{core-equivalents} (CE): 1 CE means one CPU core was busy continuously
over the measurement window.  Total busy CE sums busy time across all CPUs;
softirq CE is reported separately as descriptive context.  Module counters
measure classified ordered-head episodes and pulled jobs, but are not
inferential endpoints.

\paragraph{Experimental hierarchy and statistics.}
Mechanism studies and the steal-budget sweep are exploratory or descriptive.
Budget selection is kept separate from both confirmations.  Each final
campaign randomizes the four treatments within a block and analyzes
within-block contrasts.  The saturated campaign declares throughput and total
busy CPU as co-primary endpoints for steal4 minus off.  Efficiency
(Gb/s per busy CE) is secondary and derived.  The matched-load campaign
declares total busy CPU as its single primary endpoint.  We use exact
two-sided sign-flip tests on paired block differences and 10,000-resample
paired bootstrap confidence intervals with the analyzer's fixed seed.
Secondary factorial contrasts use Holm adjustment.  Every final run records
knob readbacks, module version, raw-result identity, per-core snapshots, and
new kernel-warning lines; the analyzer rejects incomplete blocks or failed
validity gates.

\subsection{Receive Steering and the Single-Tunnel Bottleneck}
\label{sec:eval-steering}

We first distinguish aggregate multi-tunnel scaling from the single-tunnel
problem studied by work stealing.  With the tested NIC's default UDP hash
fields (\texttt{sd}, source and destination addresses), eight tunnels between
the same two hosts concentrated receive work on one queue and delivered
4.08--4.13 Gb/s.  The hottest CPU was fully busy and approximately 97.6\% of
its time was softirq.  Adding UDP source and destination ports to the hash
(\texttt{sdfn}) distributed the tunnels across roughly eight receive cores and
delivered 8.99 Gb/s (Figure~\ref{fig:steering}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{steering_baseline.pdf}
  \caption{Receive-side CPU distribution under the tested NIC hash settings.
  Adding UDP ports spreads eight distinct tunnels; it does not split one
  tunnel's outer five-tuple. Aggregate throughput values are the two-run ranges
  recorded by the committed harness summary.}
  \label{fig:steering}
\end{figure}

Port-aware hashing therefore addresses a multi-tunnel placement bottleneck.
It cannot create several RX flows from one tunnel with fixed outer addresses
and ports.  In the uncapped single-tunnel confirmation, mean softirq
consumption remained 0.990--0.994 CE across conditions.  That near-one-core
receive path is the regime in which changing work performed by the NAPI CPU can
alter the binding limit.

\subsection{Wake-Side Mechanism Validation}
\label{sec:eval-wake}

The two-sided wake intervention substantially changes poll composition.  In a
15-s peer sweep with port-aware receive steering, the stock wasted-poll
fraction was 27.0\%, 27.3\%, 26.8\%, and 27.5\% for 8, 16, 32, and 64 peers.
Enabling both wake mechanisms reduced those values to 14.8\%, 13.8\%, 13.1\%,
and 14.4\%, respectively.  The reduction is real but does not increase with
peer count in this real-NIC setup.

To test whether the intervention responds to the interval during which the
head remains incomplete, we injected 0, 1, 2, 5, or 10 us of busy-wait delay
per decrypt.  The capped generator delivered approximately 0.96--1.05 Gb/s;
we do not treat its nominal 2-Gb/s setting as delivered load.  Across five
repetitions per cell, the median stock wasted fraction remained near
33--35\%.  The both treatment reduced it from 34.4\% to 15.2\% at native cost
and from 34.6\% to 3.8\% at 10 us (Figure~\ref{fig:wake-delay}).  The removed
fraction increased monotonically from 56\% to 89\% across the tested doses.
This is mechanism evidence that the wake paths act on premature polls; it does
not establish an application-level benefit.

\subsection{Why Premature Polls Are Too Cheap}
\label{sec:eval-poll-cost}

Poll counts alone do not reveal their CPU importance.  We therefore summed the
duration of receive-poll invocations returning zero with a BPF kretprobe over
30-s windows.  After excluding cold windows according to the prespecified
per-cell provenance, a measured wasted poll occupied approximately
1.14--1.36 us.  Probe overhead makes this an upper-bound estimate of the
uninstrumented function.

At native decrypt cost, stock executed 483,116 wasted polls whose measured
duration summed to 656.7 ms.  That population consumed 0.0219 CE over the
window.  At the 10-us injected delay, a comparable stock window consumed
0.0231 CE.  Figure~\ref{fig:poll-cost} contrasts per-poll duration with the
aggregate budget.  The wake treatment can remove many events yet the complete
stock population occupies only about two hundredths of a core.  This scale is
consistent with the absence of a clear throughput or aggregate-CPU payoff in
the wake-mechanism campaigns, but it does not prove that the wake changes are
redundant.

\subsection{Characterizing Ordered-Head Blocking}
\label{sec:eval-blocking}

The cost measurement motivated a different question: how long is delivery
blocked behind a head known to be \textsc{uncrypted}, rather than how long one
unsuccessful poll executes?  The in-module classifier separates empty-queue
episodes from episodes opened on an \textsc{uncrypted} head.  We use the
spread-guaranteed rerun with three 30-s repetitions at native, 5-us, and 10-us
decrypt delay.

Across those conditions, the classifier recorded approximately 150,000 to
298,000 \textsc{uncrypted}-head episodes per window.  Their repetition means
ranged from 35 to 94 us, and most observations fell in the 16--128-us bucket
(Figure~\ref{fig:blocking}).  At most about 1\% exceeded 1 ms; the empty-queue
class, not the encrypted-head class, carried most of the millisecond tail.
Thus the blocked interval is tens of times larger than the approximately
1.2-us cost of asking prematurely.  These episode durations delimit a
scheduling opportunity; they are not packet or application latency.

  \centering

  \caption{Count and mean duration of classified
  \textsc{uncrypted}-head episodes in the spread-guaranteed rerun. Each marker
  is one 30-s repetition; the evidence is descriptive.}
  \label{fig:blocking}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Selecting the Steal Budget}
\label{sec:eval-selection}

An initial four-condition experiment established feasibility: the stealing
path pulled device-ring jobs and reduced classified blocked wall time from
approximately 9.50 s to 0.44 s per 30-s window.  Because that comparison used
four repetitions and budget eight, we use it only as mechanism evidence.

We selected the final budget in a separate uncapped single-tunnel sweep:
values 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 16, each with five shuffled 20-s repetitions.
Table~\ref{tab:steal-sweep} reports the exploratory means relative to off.
Each value was compared with off using an exact independent permutation test
with 252 allocations; the five throughput comparisons used a Bonferroni
threshold of 0.01.  Budget four was the only value whose throughput comparison
passed that threshold: +4.15\%, $p=0.0079$.  Its CPU estimate was -3.20\%
($p=0.0159$), and its derived efficiency estimate was +7.60\%
($p=0.0079$).  We selected four because it combined the largest observed
throughput and efficiency means with a small per-pass bound.  The sweep neither
proves global optimality nor supplies the final effect estimate.

\subsection{Saturated Paired Confirmation}
\label{sec:eval-gatea}

The uncapped confirmation (Gate A) comprises 12 randomized paired blocks.
Every block contains off, both, steal4, and bsteal4 once in a newly shuffled
order; all 48 runs use 30-s single-tunnel windows.  The campaign ran on the
same CloudLab instantiation as the selection study after a controlled reset.
All knob, structure, raw-identity, per-core, and dmesg gates passed.

Figure~\ref{fig:gate-a} displays every paired steal4-minus-off block.  Mean
throughput increased by 0.0818 Gb/s, or 1.96\%, with bootstrap CI95
[+0.0343,+0.1297] Gb/s.  Nine of 12 blocks were favorable and the exact
two-sided sign-flip test gives $p=0.0103$.  Mean total busy CPU fell by
0.1757 CE, or 3.66\%, with CI95 [-0.2038,-0.1459] CE.  All 12 blocks were
favorable and the exact test reaches its two-sided resolution floor,
$p=0.000488$.  Both declared co-primary endpoints therefore pass in the
favorable direction.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{gate_a_paired.pdf}
  \caption{Saturated paired confirmation. Each point is the within-block
  steal4-minus-off difference; the diamond and horizontal interval show the
  mean and paired-bootstrap CI95. Throughput and total busy CPU are
  co-primary.}
  \label{fig:gate-a}
\end{figure*}

The secondary derived efficiency increased by 0.0506 Gb/s/CE, or 5.83\%,
with CI95 [+0.0427,+0.0582], favorable in all 12 blocks
($p=0.000488$).  It is not a third primary endpoint.  In the Holm-adjusted
composition family, both minus off showed no detected throughput effect
(-0.13\%, raw $p=0.6035$) and a small detected CPU reduction
(-0.62\%, Holm $p=0.0322$).  No incremental throughput, CPU, or efficiency
effect was detected for bsteal4 over steal4.  Those non-detections neither
establish treatment equivalence nor make the wake mechanisms redundant.

\subsection{Matched-Load CPU Confirmation}
\label{sec:eval-gateb}

The matched-load confirmation (Gate B) asks the narrower question ``same
delivered bytes, less total CPU?''  It removes the invalid closed-loop latency
probe and runs in CPU-only mode.  Eight randomized blocks provide 32 complete
60-s windows.  The application cap is 3.58 Gb/s, calibrated so that delivered
inner traffic measured from \texttt{wg0} receive bytes targets 3.8 Gb/s over
the exact CPU window.

All validity gates passed.  Individual delivered loads span
3.8012--3.8027 Gb/s; the maximum target deviation is approximately 0.071\%,
and the worst required within-block paired mismatch is 0.0316\%.  These values
are far inside the predeclared 5\% absolute and 1.5\% paired gates.
The artifact set contains 1,280 unique block/position/CPU tuples, exactly 32
raw \texttt{iperf3} JSON files, no Sockperf file, and no relevant dmesg record.

Figure~\ref{fig:gate-b} shows both the load check and all eight primary paired
CPU differences.  The steal4-minus-off mean is -0.047 CE (-1.17\%), with
CI95 [-0.245,+0.134] CE.  Three of eight blocks favor steal4 and the exact
test gives $p=0.6562$.  We therefore detect no favorable total-CPU effect at
the tested matched load.  The interval includes both a moderate saving and a
small cost, so the experiment does not establish equivalence or absence of an
effect.  No secondary contrast was detected after Holm adjustment.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{gate_b_matched.pdf}
  \caption{Matched-load CPU confirmation. Left: all 32 exact-window delivered
  loads. Right: the eight paired steal4-minus-off total-CPU differences with
  mean and CI95. The interval spans benefit and cost; the result is a
  non-detection, not equivalence.}
  \label{fig:gate-b}
\end{figure*}

Descriptive counters show that the mechanism still activated below the
uncapped ceiling.  In the two conditions without wake suppression, the mean
classified \textsc{uncrypted}-head count fell from approximately 743,942 per
window with off to 10,021 with steal4, a 98.7\% reduction.  Steal4 pulled
approximately 2.32 million decrypt jobs per window.  These counters demonstrate
mechanism activation and a changed blocked-head population; they are not
latency measurements or statistically tested performance endpoints.

\subsection{Cross-Regime Interpretation}
\label{sec:eval-cross-regime}

The two confirmations expose different operating contexts.  In the uncapped
single-tunnel campaign, condition means for RX softirq occupancy are
approximately 0.99 CE, and stealing both lowers total CPU and raises the
throughput ceiling.  At the exactly matched 3.8-Gb/s load, condition means are
approximately 0.81--0.86 softirq CE; classified blocking is almost eliminated,
but the total-CPU contrast is not detected.

This pattern is consistent with the stolen work producing a detectable payoff
as the pinned RX/softirq core approaches its binding limit.  It does not
formally establish a saturation-by-treatment interaction: the campaigns have
different load controls, block counts, durations, and diagnostic settings.
Nor does it establish saturation as the cause of the different estimates or
an exclusive operating threshold for the treatment.

\subsection{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:eval-limitations}

\paragraph{External validity.}
Measurements cover one server class, 10-GbE NIC family, Linux 5.15 module, and
small set of tunnel/load configurations.  Receive hashing and scheduler costs
can differ on other NICs and kernels.  The original steering throughput
summary is preserved as committed experimental documentation rather than its
machine-readable harness output; the per-core CSV evidence is committed.

\paragraph{Replication and dependence.}
Randomized blocks control temporal drift within each campaign, but Gate A and
Gate B ran on the same CloudLab instantiation after controlled resets.  They
are paired confirmations, not independent fresh-node replications.  The
cross-regime comparison is descriptive rather than a pooled or factorial
interaction test.

\paragraph{Instrumentation.}
The cost probe adds overhead, so its per-poll duration is an upper bound.
Diagnostic arrays are racy global counters suitable for ratios and
order-of-magnitude mechanism evidence.  Gate A disables them; Gate B enables
them uniformly, but only off and steal4 have comparable classifier semantics
because the wake treatments also change episode formation.

\paragraph{Latency.}
A preliminary loaded-tunnel smoke used closed-loop UDP Sockperf.  An unanswered
packet could freeze the probe, producing condition-dependent valid durations
and insufficient p99.9 support.  Those distributions are withdrawn.  We make
no user-visible latency claim; an open-loop or loss-tolerant study is required.

\paragraph{Safety and fairness.}
The campaigns and two 15-minute stealing soaks recorded no relevant kernel
warning, tunnel collapse, or lost handshake.  This did not reveal a reliability
problem in the tested regimes, but it is not proof of production safety.
Synchronous softirq crypto is not charged to NAPI's delivery budget, and this
study does not quantify per-peer fairness or scheduler-latency effects.
