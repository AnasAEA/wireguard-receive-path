\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

This section builds the model a reader needs to follow the mechanism. It
introduces the receive path, the two orderings that collide on it, the
single-tunnel setting in which the collision binds, and the measurement unit we
use for CPU. Readers familiar with WireGuard internals may skip to
Section~\ref{sec:background-inversion}.

\subsection{WireGuard Receive Processing}
\label{sec:background-recv}

A modern NIC exposes several hardware receive queues. It hashes each incoming
packet's flow identifier and steers the packet to a queue, so that different
flows are served by different CPUs~\cite{linux-napi,salim2001beyond}. Linux
drains a queue with NAPI: after the first interrupt, the kernel masks further
interrupts for that queue and repeatedly runs a poll function in softirq
context, delivering packets in batches until the queue drains or a budget is
reached. Softirq is a deferred, high-priority kernel context that runs after
hardware interrupts and cannot sleep.

WireGuard adds decryption between the NIC and the network stack, and it uses two
distinct structures to do so (Figure~\ref{fig:recv-overview}). When a data
packet arrives, WireGuard appends it to a \emph{per-peer ordered queue} in
arrival order and marks it \textsc{uncrypted}. It also publishes the same packet
to a \emph{device-wide decrypt ring}, a single lock-protected ring shared by the
whole WireGuard device~\cite{wireguard-linux}. Per-CPU \emph{decrypt workers}
consume the ring in parallel, authenticate and decrypt each packet with
ChaCha20-Poly1305~\cite{rfc8439}, and mark it \textsc{crypted}, or
\textsc{dead} on failure. The two structures serve deliberately different
purposes: the device ring chooses \emph{which CPU} decrypts a packet, while the
per-peer queue records the order in which packets must \emph{leave}. The ring
does not preserve packet order, and it never needs to, because ordering is the
per-peer queue's responsibility.

\subsection{Parallel Decryption and Ordered Delivery}
\label{sec:background-order}

Two orderings coexist on this path. \emph{Arrival order} is fixed when a packet
enters the per-peer queue and does not change. \emph{Completion order} is the
order in which workers finish decrypting, and it is set by per-CPU scheduling
and load, not by arrival. Because consecutive packets of one tunnel are handed
to different workers, they decrypt concurrently and can complete in any order.

Delivery, however, is strictly ordered. The per-peer queue has exactly one
consumer, the tunnel's RX/NAPI poll. The poll examines the \emph{ordered head},
the next packet the queue is allowed to release, and removes it only after an
acquire load observes a terminal state: a \textsc{crypted} head proceeds to the
network stack, and a \textsc{dead} head is drained. An \textsc{uncrypted} head
stops the poll. A later packet that has already completed cannot pass an
incomplete head, because releasing it would deliver the tunnel's packets out of
order.

\subsection{Execution-Order Inversion}
\label{sec:background-inversion}

When completion order diverges from arrival order, the poll can be runnable yet
unable to make progress. Figure~\ref{fig:eoi-timeline} traces the smallest case.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{c2_eoi_timeline.pdf}
  \Description{A timeline with two decrypt lanes and one delivery lane. Packet
  P1 arrives first and P2 second, but P2 finishes decrypting before P1. When
  P2 completes, the delivery poll runs and finds the head P1 still encrypted, so
  it delivers nothing. A shaded interval marks the blocked window that lasts
  until P1 finishes, after which both packets are delivered in order.}
  \caption{Execution-order inversion for two packets of one tunnel. \texttt{P2}
  completes before the ordered head \texttt{P1}, so the poll that \texttt{P2}
  wakes delivers nothing; delivery stays blocked until \texttt{P1} completes.
  Holding the ready \texttt{P2} behind \texttt{P1} is correct ordered delivery,
  not an error.}
  \label{fig:eoi-timeline}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Packet \texttt{P1} arrives first and becomes the ordered head.
  \item Packet \texttt{P2} arrives second and is queued behind it.
  \item Both packets are published to the device ring and dispatched to parallel
    decrypt workers.
  \item \texttt{P2}'s worker finishes first, so \texttt{P2} becomes
    \textsc{crypted} while \texttt{P1} is still \textsc{uncrypted}.
  \item \texttt{P2} is ready, but it cannot be delivered: it sits behind the
    incomplete head \texttt{P1}.
  \item The receive poll reaches \texttt{P1}, observes \textsc{uncrypted}, and
    stops without delivering anything.
  \item Meanwhile, pending decrypt jobs may still remain in the device-wide
    ring, including \texttt{P1}'s own job if no worker has claimed it yet.
  \item The blocked poll is the one context that has noticed the stall, and it
    is idle. This is the opportunity that bounded work stealing exploits.
\end{enumerate}

We call this pattern head-of-line blocking under an execution-order inversion.
Two points are worth stating plainly, because both guard against a natural
misreading. First, stock WireGuard handles \texttt{P2} correctly: holding a
ready follower behind an incomplete head is exactly what ordered delivery
requires, not a bug. Second, the stall is not caused by \texttt{P1} being
inherently slow to decrypt; as Section~\ref{sec:background-wake} shows, the head
usually waits to be \emph{scheduled} onto a worker. The inefficiency is a
scheduling one: useful decrypt work is pending, and the context that needs it
completed is doing nothing.

\subsection{Receive Steering and the Single-Tunnel Bottleneck}
\label{sec:background-steering}

Receive steering determines how much of this matters. Flow hashing distributes
distinct flows across receive queues and cores, and receive-side scaling has a
long line of work on placing connections to improve
locality~\cite{linux-napi,pesterev2012affinity}. For WireGuard, steering acts on
the \emph{outer} UDP flow. Several tunnels between the same two hosts share a
source and destination IP address, so the default address-only hash sends them
all to one queue; adding the UDP ports to the hash spreads them across cores. In
our testbed, the default hash concentrated eight tunnels on a single receive
core at about 4.1~Gb/s, while a port-aware hash spread them across roughly eight
cores at about 9.0~Gb/s (Section~\ref{sec:eval-steering}).

That result is about placing \emph{many} tunnels; it cannot split \emph{one}. A
single tunnel is a single outer five-tuple, so every packet hashes to the same
queue and its ordered delivery runs in one RX/NAPI context regardless of how
many cores are idle. In the uncapped single-tunnel experiments, that context's
softirq processing stayed near one core-equivalent across conditions. The
single-heavy-tunnel case, common in site-to-site links, is therefore the regime
where the receive core is the binding resource and where changing the work that
context performs can move the ceiling.

\subsection{From Premature Wakes to Pending Decrypt Work}
\label{sec:background-wake}

There are two ways an inversion wastes the receive core, and they cost very
differently. The visible symptom is \emph{premature polls}: a completed non-head
packet wakes the poll, which runs, finds the head still \textsc{uncrypted}, and
delivers nothing. Wake-side changes target these polls, and they work. Two
composable changes, described in Section~\ref{sec:design-wake}, cut the
wasted-poll fraction from about 27\% to 13--15\%, and the share they remove
grows as decryption is slowed, which ties the effect directly to the inversion.

Yet removing premature polls does not help throughput or aggregate CPU on this
hardware, because each premature poll is cheap. Summing the duration of every
empty poll over a measurement window shows that the entire stock population
occupies only about $0.02$ core-equivalents
(Section~\ref{sec:eval-poll-cost}). The wake-side changes remove many events,
but the events are individually inexpensive, so the reclaimed CPU is far below
the run-to-run noise. This is a genuine result rather than a failed fix: it
locates the cost, and it redirects attention to a different quantity.

That quantity is the time delivery spends \emph{blocked} behind an incomplete
head. A per-episode classifier that separates empty-queue stalls from
incomplete-head stalls shows the incomplete-head intervals last tens of
microseconds, with means of roughly 35--94~us
(Section~\ref{sec:eval-blocking}). The head is not slow to decrypt; it is slow
to be scheduled onto a worker, and the blocked poll waits out that scheduling
delay. Figure~\ref{fig:investigation} summarizes the reasoning: premature polls
are real but cheap, whereas the blocked interval is longer-lived and is where a
direct intervention can act. Bounded work stealing is that intervention. Rather
than merely avoiding a wake, it advances the pending decrypt work that the
delivery consumer is waiting for. Figure~\ref{fig:steal-panels} contrasts the
stock stall with a stealing pass; Section~\ref{sec:design-steal} specifies the
mechanism.

\subsection{Measuring Receive-Side CPU}
\label{sec:background-ce}

Throughput alone cannot show where receive-side cost goes, so we report CPU in
\emph{core-equivalents} (CE). One core-equivalent represents one CPU core busy
continuously over the measurement interval; four CE can mean four continuously
busy cores or an equivalent aggregate occupancy spread across more cores. We
compute CE from per-CPU busy time over the exact measurement window, sum it
across all logical CPUs for total busy CE, and report softirq CE separately as
descriptive context. This unit lets us compare the cost of the same delivered
bytes under different treatments, and it is the endpoint on which the
confirmatory experiments in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} rest.
