\begin{abstract}
%% ============================================================

WireGuard, a modern VPN integrated into the Linux kernel, achieves only
19.2\% of available bandwidth under sustained multi-gigabit traffic with
1,000 concurrent clients. Prior work identified an Execution Order Inversion
(EoI)---a structural conflict between WireGuard's packet decryption pipeline
and its packet reassembly scheduling mechanism---and proposed moving packet
reassembly to a dedicated background thread as a fix, recovering 4.7$\times$
throughput. Through source-level analysis, we show that this fix reduces the
cost of each wasted reassembly invocation but does not reduce their number.
Under concurrent decryption across $N$ CPU cores, the probability that a
reassembly invocation finds useful work is at most $1/N$: on an 8-core
machine, 87.5\% of invocations are structurally wasted. We propose a
six-line conditional check at the scheduling site that eliminates the dominant
source of wasted invocations, prove its correctness under concurrent access,
and evaluate it on an ARM platform (Apple M1~Pro).

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) are essential infrastructure for secure
communication in enterprise and cloud environments. WireGuard~\cite{donenfeld2017}
is a modern VPN implementation integrated into the Linux kernel since
version~5.6 (2020). It is built around simplicity and correctness: a minimal
codebase, the ChaCha20-Poly1305 authenticated encryption
algorithm~\cite{rfc8439}, and a cryptographic handshake based on the Noise
protocol framework~\cite{noise}. Its straightforward design and competitive
performance have made it the preferred replacement for older VPN solutions
such as IPsec and OpenVPN.

At multi-gigabit throughput, however, WireGuard exhibits unexpected
performance degradation on multi-core systems.
\citeauthor{mounah2025}~\cite{mounah2025} measured a throughput collapse to
approximately 19.2\% of line rate and 94\% CPU utilization under sustained
25~Gbps traffic with 1,000 concurrent clients. The authors identified the
source of this degradation as an interaction between WireGuard's concurrent
packet decryption pipeline and the Linux kernel's Generic Receive Offload
(GRO) scheduling mechanism---a phenomenon they term an Execution Order
Inversion (EoI). Their proposed fix, which moves GRO execution from a
high-priority software interrupt context to a dedicated background thread
running at normal scheduling priority, yields a 4.7$\times$ throughput
improvement~\cite{mounah2025}.

Despite this result, the fix is incomplete. Moving GRO to a background thread
reduces the cost of each scheduling event but does not reduce the number of
such events. The root cause---WireGuard unconditionally schedules GRO after
every decrypted packet, regardless of whether the packet queue is in a state
where GRO can make any progress---is left unaddressed. Under concurrent
decryption across $N$ CPU cores, the probability that GRO finds useful work
on any given invocation is at most $1/N$. On an 8-core machine, at least
87.5\% of GRO invocations are structurally wasted. This is not a scheduling
priority problem; it is a trigger logic problem.

This work presents a source-level analysis of WireGuard's receive pipeline
and a targeted fix for the root cause. The analysis traces the exact sequence
of events that leads to wasted GRO invocations, identifies the structural
conditions under which they occur, and provides a probabilistic model
predicting their frequency as a function of CPU count. The proposed fix adds
a six-line conditional check at the GRO scheduling site: GRO is invoked only
when the head of the per-peer receive queue is ready for processing. We prove
this check correct under concurrent access and evaluate it experimentally.

\smallskip\noindent This paper addresses the following research questions:
\begin{description}
  \item[RQ1.] What is the exact mechanism that causes GRO to be invoked
    wastefully in WireGuard's receive path?
  \item[RQ2.] Does the fix by \citeauthor{mounah2025} eliminate the root
    cause of the overhead, or only reduce its per-invocation cost?
  \item[RQ3.] Can a conditional check at the scheduling site eliminate the
    dominant source of wasted invocations without breaking correctness or
    packet ordering?
  \item[RQ4.] Does the problem manifest with the same intensity on ARM as
    on x86-64?
\end{description}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Scope and limitations.} We focus on WireGuard's
data receive path. The handshake path uses a separate, blocking workqueue and
is not addressed here. Measurements are conducted on ARM (Apple M1~Pro,
Fedora Asahi Remix); a cross-architecture comparison on x86-64 via dedicated
hardware is left as future work.

Section~\ref{sec:rootcause} presents the root cause analysis.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{WireGuard's Receive Pipeline}

WireGuard's receive path consists of three ordered stages. Stage~1 runs
inside the kernel's UDP receive handler, which executes in \emph{bottom-half}
(BH) context---a deferred, high-priority execution mode triggered by hardware
interrupts. It de-encapsulates incoming UDP datagrams and dispatches them for
decryption. Stage~2 performs ChaCha20-Poly1305 decryption inside a
\emph{workqueue}: a kernel-managed pool of background threads that run at
normal scheduler priority. Stage~3 reassembles decrypted packets and hands
them to the network stack via the Generic Receive Offload (GRO) mechanism,
which executes as a \emph{softirq} (a high-priority deferred interrupt
handler).

Packets flow through two structurally independent queues. The \emph{global
device ring} (\texttt{ptr\_ring}) holds packets from all peers mixed together;
it determines which CPU core decrypts each packet, assigned by approximate
round-robin across online cores. The \emph{per-peer RX queue}
(\texttt{prev\_queue}) holds packets from a single peer in strict arrival
order; it determines the order in which packets are delivered to the network
stack. These two roles are separated by design: delivery order is established
when a packet is inserted into the per-peer queue (Phase~1, state set to
\textsc{uncrypted}), before it is dispatched to any CPU (Phase~2). A worker
atomically upgrades the state to \textsc{crypted} or \textsc{dead} upon
completing decryption.

\subsection{NAPI, GRO, and Softirqs}

The Linux NAPI interface is a polling mechanism for network receive
processing. Each WireGuard peer has one \texttt{napi\_struct} instance.
Calling \texttt{napi\_schedule} raises the \texttt{NET\_RX\_SOFTIRQ} software
interrupt and sets an internal scheduling flag; a second call while the flag
is set is a no-op. When the softirq fires, WireGuard's poll function
\texttt{wg\_packet\_rx\_poll} walks the per-peer RX queue from its head,
delivers all contiguous non-\textsc{uncrypted} packets to the network stack
via GRO, and stops at the first \textsc{uncrypted} entry. The poll budget is
64~packets (the kernel default). When the poll completes without exhausting
the budget, \texttt{napi\_complete\_done} clears the scheduling flag,
re-arming the NAPI instance for future invocations.

The softirq fires at the earliest \texttt{spin\_unlock\_bh} call following
\texttt{napi\_schedule}---in WireGuard's case, at the next iteration of the
decrypt worker's packet consumption loop.

\subsection{Linux Workqueues}

Workqueues are the kernel's standard mechanism for deferring work to
normal-priority background threads. WireGuard's decrypt workqueue
(\texttt{packet\_crypt\_wq}) is created with the \texttt{WQ\_PERCPU} flag,
which provisions one worker thread per online CPU core. Work items are
dispatched to a specific core via \texttt{queue\_work\_on(cpu, wq, work)}.
Workers run at \texttt{SCHED\_NORMAL} (standard Linux scheduling priority)
and can be preempted by softirqs at any BH re-enable point.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:sota}
%% ============================================================

\citeauthor{mounah2025}~\cite{mounah2025} provide the most direct prior work.
They measure WireGuard's throughput collapse under sustained 25~Gbps load with
1,000 concurrent clients and identify the cause as an Execution Order
Inversion: the WireGuard decrypt workqueue continuously preempts itself by
raising \texttt{NET\_RX\_SOFTIRQ} via \texttt{napi\_schedule}, which runs the
GRO handler at softirq priority, which in turn interrupts the decrypt workers.
Their fix moves \texttt{napi\_schedule} to a dedicated \texttt{gro\_wq}
workqueue running at \texttt{SCHED\_NORMAL} priority, breaking the preemption
cycle and yielding a 4.7$\times$ throughput improvement. An alternative using
dedicated kernel threads achieves a 4$\times$ improvement with 65\% tail
latency reduction.

Our analysis shows that their fix addresses the \emph{priority} of GRO
invocations but not their \emph{frequency}. The fundamental trigger---an
unconditional \texttt{napi\_schedule} after every decrypted packet, regardless
of queue readiness---is unchanged by either of their variants. The two fixes
are orthogonal: their fix reduces per-invocation cost; ours reduces invocation
count. Combining them is expected to yield additional benefit.

WireGuard's design and cryptographic foundations are described in the original
paper by \citeauthor{donenfeld2017}~\cite{donenfeld2017}. The NAPI polling
mechanism and GRO are standard Linux kernel interfaces; their interaction with
workqueues under high packet rates is the subject of ongoing kernel
development.

\section{Root Cause Analysis}
\label{sec:rootcause}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Two Independent Queues}

WireGuard maintains two structurally independent queues per device
(Figure~\ref{fig:queues}). The \emph{global device ring} is a
\texttt{ptr\_ring} shared across all peers; it is the dispatch queue for the
decrypt workqueue. When a UDP datagram arrives, Stage~1 inserts the packet
into both the global ring (for dispatch to a decrypt worker) and the per-peer
queue (to establish delivery order), in that order.

The \emph{per-peer RX queue} (\texttt{prev\_queue}, a multi-producer
single-consumer queue) holds packets from a single peer in strict arrival
order. Each entry carries a state flag (\textsc{uncrypted}, \textsc{crypted},
or \textsc{dead}) that is written exactly once, atomically, by the decrypt
worker assigned to that packet. The GRO poll function
(\texttt{wg\_packet\_rx\_poll}) is the sole consumer: it walks the queue from
the head and delivers all contiguous non-\textsc{uncrypted} packets, stopping
at the first \textsc{uncrypted} entry.

This separation of roles has a critical implication: the global ring
determines \emph{which CPU} processes each packet, and this assignment is
independent of the per-peer order. Packets from the same peer are routinely
dispatched to different CPU cores and decrypted concurrently.

\subsection{Concurrent Decryption and the Head-Packet Disadvantage}

\texttt{wg\_cpumask\_next\_online} ({\small\texttt{queueing.h:115--119}})
assigns packets to CPU cores by approximate round-robin over the set of online
cores. For a peer with $N$ packets in flight, packet~0 (the head, oldest in
arrival order) goes to CPU~$c_0$, packet~1 to CPU~$c_1$, and so on. All $N$
workers run concurrently (Figure~\ref{fig:concurrent}).

Suppose CPU~$c_2$ completes decryption of packet~2 first. It marks packet~2
\textsc{crypted} and calls \texttt{napi\_schedule}. The GRO poll fires and
examines the head of the per-peer queue: packet~0, which is still
\textsc{uncrypted}. The poll immediately returns with \texttt{work\_done = 0}.
Packet~2, though ready, is unreachable until packet~0 is delivered first.

The head packet (packet~0) carries a systematic disadvantage. CPU~$c_0$ may
be finishing a previous peer's packet when packet~0 arrives, causing it to
start decryption later than CPUs $c_1, \ldots, c_{N-1}$ start their
respective packets. This makes $1/N$ an upper bound on the probability that
the head finishes first, not an average.

\subsection{The Unconditional Trigger}
\label{subsec:trigger}

The scheduling call is at \texttt{wg\_queue\_enqueue\_per\_peer\_rx}
({\small\texttt{queueing.h:188--198}}):

\begin{lstlisting}[language=C]
atomic_set_release(&PACKET_CB(skb)->state, state);
napi_schedule(&peer->napi);
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent The \texttt{napi\_schedule} call is unconditional. It fires for
every decrypted packet regardless of whether the head of the per-peer queue is
ready for delivery. The softirq raises \texttt{NET\_RX\_SOFTIRQ} at the next
\texttt{spin\_unlock\_bh} in the decrypt worker's loop; \texttt{wg\_packet\_rx\_poll}
runs, finds the head \textsc{uncrypted}, and returns immediately with
\texttt{work\_done = 0}. The worker then proceeds to the next packet, calls
\texttt{napi\_schedule} again, and the cycle repeats
(Figure~\ref{fig:loop}).

\subsection{Probabilistic Model}
\label{subsec:model}

For GRO to deliver packet~$k$ (at position $k$ in the per-peer queue), all
packets at positions $0, \ldots, k$ must be \textsc{crypted} and the poll must
arrive after all of them are ready. For a single packet (the head, $k=0$),
GRO makes progress if and only if packet~0 is \textsc{crypted} when the poll
fires. Under $N$-way concurrent decryption with uniform decryption times, the
probability that packet~0 is the \emph{first} to complete is $1/N$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
$N$ cores & $P(\text{useful poll})$ & wasted fraction \\
\midrule
2  & $1/2$    & 50\%    \\
4  & $1/4$    & 75\%    \\
8  & $1/8$    & 87.5\%  \\
16 & $1/16$   & 93.75\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Two structural amplifiers make this worse in practice. First, the head-packet
disadvantage described above makes $1/N$ an overestimate of the useful-poll
probability. Second, \texttt{napi\_schedule} fires per-packet, not per-batch:
every one of $N$ concurrent workers independently triggers a poll after
finishing its packet. The cumulative effect is the 94\% CPU saturation
measured by \citeauthor{mounah2025}.

\section{Proposed Fix}
\label{sec:fix}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Condition Design}

GRO makes progress on peer~$p$'s queue if and only if the head entry is not
\textsc{uncrypted}. The fix therefore guards the \texttt{napi\_schedule} call
with a check of the head entry's state: if the head is still
\textsc{uncrypted}, skip the schedule.

Reading the head entry directly is not safe from worker context because
\texttt{wg\_prev\_queue\_peek}, the queue's consumer-side read function,
maintains a single-consumer invariant and would corrupt the GRO consumer's
peeked-entry cache if called from a worker. The safe alternative is to read
\texttt{READ\_ONCE(peer->rx\_queue.tail)}---the last entry inserted by any
producer. The \texttt{tail} pointer is written only by the consumer
(\texttt{wg\_prev\_queue\_dequeue}, {\small\texttt{queueing.c:87,92,101}});
reading it from worker context is safe as a hint. There is one additional
case: when the queue is on the sentinel boundary
(\texttt{tail == \&peer->rx\_queue.empty}), the fix conservatively schedules.

\subsection{The Diff}

The complete change is six lines in \texttt{drivers/net/wireguard/queueing.h}:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=C, caption={Conditional \texttt{napi\_schedule}
  (queueing.h, André Freyssinet)}]
 static inline void
 wg_queue_enqueue_per_peer_rx(struct sk_buff *skb,
                               enum packet_state state)
 {
     struct wg_peer *peer = wg_peer_get(PACKET_PEER(skb));
+    struct sk_buff *tail;

     atomic_set_release(&PACKET_CB(skb)->state, state);
-    napi_schedule(&peer->napi);
+
+    tail = READ_ONCE(peer->rx_queue.tail);
+    if (tail == (struct sk_buff *)&peer->rx_queue.empty ||
+        atomic_read(&PACKET_CB(tail)->state) !=
+                PACKET_STATE_UNCRYPTED)
+            napi_schedule(&peer->napi);
+
     wg_peer_put(peer);
 }
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Correctness}

There are four cases after worker~$w$ marks packet~$k$ \textsc{crypted}
(Figure~\ref{fig:fix}):

\begin{description}
  \item[Case A.] $k$ is the head; now \textsc{crypted}. The tail read
    returns a non-\textsc{uncrypted} state. \texttt{napi\_schedule} fires.
    GRO finds packet~$k$ at the head and delivers it.

  \item[Case B.] $k$ is not the head; the head is already \textsc{crypted}
    or \textsc{dead} (delivered by a prior poll, so tail is past it).
    \texttt{napi\_schedule} fires. GRO delivers from the head.

  \item[Case C.] $k$ is not the head; the head is \textsc{uncrypted}.
    \texttt{napi\_schedule} is skipped. GRO would return \texttt{work\_done = 0}
    anyway. The wasted invocation is eliminated.

  \item[Stub.] Queue is on the sentinel boundary. \texttt{napi\_schedule}
    fires conservatively; no progress is lost.
\end{description}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stale-read safety.} If worker~$w_1$ reads the
head state as \textsc{uncrypted} but worker~$w_0$ has just set it to
\textsc{crypted}, $w_1$ skips \texttt{napi\_schedule}. However, $w_0$ reads
\textsc{crypted} and calls \texttt{napi\_schedule}. No packet is stranded;
liveness is preserved.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Memory ordering.} \texttt{atomic\_read} (relaxed)
is correct here. This is a hint, not a synchronization point; the ordering
guarantee on \texttt{atomic\_set\_release} in the producer and the
single-consumer discipline of \texttt{wg\_prev\_queue\_dequeue} are unchanged.
A relaxed read avoids an unnecessary memory barrier.

\subsection{Residual Limitation}

A narrow timing window remains. Suppose GRO delivers packets 0 and~1, then
stops because packet~2 is \textsc{uncrypted} (\texttt{work\_done = 2}).
Between GRO stopping and \texttt{napi\_complete\_done} clearing
\texttt{NAPI\_STATE\_SCHED}, worker~$w_2$ marks packet~2 \textsc{crypted},
reads its own tail as \textsc{crypted}, and calls \texttt{napi\_schedule}.
Because \texttt{NAPI\_STATE\_SCHED} is still set, the call is a no-op.
\texttt{napi\_complete\_done} then clears the flag. No subsequent worker
reschedules, and packets 2 onward wait until the next incoming packet
re-triggers the condition.

This is not a correctness issue: under sustained high-throughput traffic, the
next packet arrives in nanoseconds. Under bursty or low-rate traffic it may
manifest as a rare tail-latency spike.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Platform.} All measurements are on an Apple M1~Pro (ARM64, 10 cores:
8 efficiency + 2 performance-class), running Fedora Asahi Remix~42, kernel
\texttt{6.19.13-400.asahi.fc44.aarch64+16k}. The patched WireGuard module
(\texttt{wireguard.ko}) was compiled from the exact running kernel source and
loaded via \texttt{insmod}.

\textbf{Module instrumentation.} The patched module exposes two sysfs
parameters: \texttt{wg\_eoi\_fix} (0 = stock, 1 = fix) and
\texttt{wg\_decrypt\_delay\_us} (artificial per-packet busy-wait to emulate
heavier crypto). This allows stock and patched behavior to be compared within
the same module instance, the same tunnel, and the same memory layout,
eliminating variance from module reloads.

\textbf{Topology.} A multi-peer loopback topology: one WireGuard server
namespace (\texttt{ns\_mp\_server}, address \texttt{10.99.0.1}) and $N$ client
namespaces (\texttt{ns\_mp\_client\_$i$}, addresses \texttt{10.99.$i$.1}).
Each client runs as a separate WireGuard peer with its own key pair, so the
server maintains $N$ independent per-peer RX queues under concurrent
decryption.

\textbf{Traffic.} iperf3 with a fixed total of 32 streams split across $N$
peers (so each peer drives $\lfloor 32/N \rfloor$ streams). Fixing total
offered load prevents peer count from being confounded with traffic volume.
Each cell runs for 30~seconds with a 5-second warm-up omitted from throughput
statistics.

\textbf{Variance control.} Each (peers, build) cell is measured 5~times;
stock and patched alternate order each run to cancel slow drift. Medians are
reported.

\textbf{Metrics.} GRO poll counts and batch size are collected via bpftrace
probes on \texttt{wg\_packet\_rx\_poll} (\texttt{retval == 0} for wasted,
\texttt{retval > 0} for useful) and a \texttt{lhist} of \texttt{retval} for
batch size. Latency is measured by 20~Hz ping from one client, with p50/p99/p99.9
percentiles computed over the measurement window.

The fix reduces wasted polls by 19.6\% even under heavy artificial delay and
2-core contention (Table~\ref{tab:regime}). Throughput is unchanged (1.78 vs
1.76~Gbps): the bottleneck has shifted to decrypt-worker throughput---the 2
cores cannot keep pace with 32 streams regardless of GRO efficiency. Tail
latency is worse under the patch in this regime (p99: 9.24 $\to$ 12.30~ms),
for the same reason as the sweep: deferred NAPI wake adds queuing time when
decryption is the bottleneck.

The throughput effect documented by \citeauthor{mounah2025} requires a
configuration where \texttt{NET\_RX\_SOFTIRQ} time is what competes with
decrypt-worker CPU, which requires real NIC hardware where the network
interface---not the CPU---is the bottleneck. On a loopback-based rig, even
with 40~µs/packet artificial delay, the constraint binds on worker throughput
rather than on softirq preemption.

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{RQ1} (mechanism): Confirmed at source level. The unconditional
\texttt{napi\_schedule} at \texttt{queueing.h:196} fires after every decrypted
packet; \texttt{wg\_packet\_rx\_poll} walks the per-peer queue and returns
\texttt{work\_done = 0} whenever the head is still \textsc{uncrypted}. This
occurs systematically when $N > 1$ workers decrypt packets from the same peer
concurrently, which is the common case.

\textbf{RQ2} (prior fix): The fix by \citeauthor{mounah2025} changes the
scheduling context of GRO from softirq to a workqueue thread. It does not
change the trigger logic; wasted invocations occur at the same frequency under
their fix.

\textbf{RQ3} (conditional guard): Confirmed effective. The guard reduces
wasted GRO polls by 9--22\% in the ecores sweep and by 20\% in the regime
test. Batch size increases in both cases, confirming that the fix correctly
defers scheduling until delivery is productive.

\textbf{RQ4} (ARM): The EoI manifests on ARM with 25--31\% of all GRO
invocations wasted under stock (comparable to what the model predicts for 8--16
concurrent workers). The patch code is structurally identical on ARM and x86
(confirmed by direct comparison of \texttt{queueing.h} between
\texttt{AsahiLinux/linux} v6.19 and \texttt{torvalds/linux} v6.1).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented a source-level analysis of the Execution Order Inversion in
WireGuard's receive pipeline and a targeted fix for its root cause. The
analysis shows that the EoI stems from an unconditional \texttt{napi\_schedule}
call after every decrypted packet, which triggers GRO polls that systematically
find nothing to deliver when the head of the per-peer queue is still
\textsc{uncrypted}. Under $N$-way concurrent decryption, the fraction of
wasted polls approaches $(N-1)/N$.

The proposed fix---a six-line conditional guard reading
\texttt{READ\_ONCE(peer->rx\_queue.tail)}---skips the schedule when the head
is known \textsc{uncrypted}. We proved correctness under stale reads and the
STUB sentinel edge case. Experimental evaluation on ARM (Apple M1~Pro) confirms
a 9--22\% reduction in wasted GRO polls across peer counts 1--32 at fixed
total load, and a 20\% reduction under artificial heavy-crypto conditions with
2-core contention. Batch size increases in both cases, confirming the
mechanism. Throughput on this loopback rig is unchanged, as expected: the
benefit is visible in poll-efficiency metrics, not in Gbps, because the M1
loopback does not saturate \texttt{NET\_RX\_SOFTIRQ}.

The fix by \citeauthor{mounah2025} and our fix are orthogonal: their fix
reduces the per-invocation cost of GRO; ours reduces the invocation count. A
combined deployment should yield additive benefit, and verifying this on
real NIC hardware (x86-64, 25~Gbps) is the natural next step.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Future work.}
(1)~Validate the throughput effect on x86-64 hardware with a real NIC (the
regime \citeauthor{mounah2025} tested).
(2)~Combine the conditional guard with the paper's workqueue-priority fix.
(3)~Explore per-batch GRO triggering as a longer-term architectural
improvement.
(4)~Analyze the handshake path, which uses a separate blocking workqueue and
may exhibit related overheads.
